Galactic Dynamo Diffusion Equation using Crank Nicholson with Ghost Zones#

This notebook will illustrate the crank nicholson difference method solving Galactic Dynamo Diffusion Equation.

Crank-Nicolson Difference method#

The implicit Crank-Nicolson difference equation of the Heat Equation is derived by discretising the

(11)#\[\begin{equation} \frac{\partial u_{ij+\frac{1}{2}}}{\partial t} = \frac{\partial^2 u_{ij+\frac{1}{2}}}{\partial x^2},\end{equation}\]

around \((x_i,t_{j+\frac{1}{2}})\) giving the difference equation

(12)#\[\begin{equation} \frac{w_{ij+1}-w_{ij}}{k}=\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{w_{i+1j+1}-2w_{ij+1}+w_{i-1j+1}}{h^2}+\frac{w_{i+1j}-2w_{ij}+w_{i-1j}}{h^2}\right). \end{equation}\]

Rearranging give the difference equation

(13)#\[\begin{equation} -rw_{i-1j+1}+(2+2r)w_{ij+1}-rw_{i+1j+1}=rw_{i-1j}+(2-2r)w_{ij}+rw_{i+1j} \end{equation}\]

for \(i=1,...N-1\) where \(r=\frac{k}{h^2}\).

Discete Grid#

The region \(\Omega\) is discretized into a uniform mesh \(\Omega_h\). In the spatial direction (\(x\)), it is divided into \(N\) steps, yielding a step size of:

(14)#\[\begin{equation} h=\frac{1-0}{N}, \end{equation}\]

resulting in discrete spatial points:

(15)#\[\begin{equation} x[i]=0+ih, \quad i=0,1,...,N. \end{equation}\]

In the temporal direction (\(t\)), it is divided into \(N_t\) steps, giving a step size of:

(16)#\[\begin{equation} k=\frac{1-0}{N_t}, \end{equation}\]

resulting in discrete temporal points:

(17)#\[\begin{equation} t[i]=0+ik, \quad k=0,...,K. \end{equation}\]

In this discretization scheme, the red dots represent unknown values, the green dots denote known boundary conditions, and the blue dots indicate known initial conditions of the Heat Equation. Additionally, ghost zones are included at the first and last indices. These ghost zones are crucial for defining derivatives at the boundary.

Ghost zones are introduced to extend the computational domain slightly beyond the physical domain. They essentially serve as buffer zones at the boundaries, enabling the application of boundary conditions and accurate computation of derivatives.

Initial and Boundary Conditions#

Neumann Boundary Conditions#

In the case of a slab surrounded by a medium of infinite electrical conductivity, where the field diffusion across the slab’s surface is suppressed (\( \sigma = 0 \)), the boundary conditions become \( \frac{\partial B_r}{\partial z} = \frac{\partial B_\phi}{\partial z} = 0 \) at \( |z| = h \). This scenario is discussed in the works of Parker (1971a) and Choudhuri (1984).

Discrete Initial and Boundary Conditions#

The discrete initial conditions are \(B[i,0]= B_o sin(\gamma z[i]\) and the discrete boundary conditions are

\[\frac{\partial B[0,j]}{\partial z} = \frac{\partial B[N,j]}{\partial z} = 0\]

where \(B[i,j]\) is the numerical approximation of \(B(x[i],t[j])\). We have now used the exact eigensolution for the initial condition, denoted by B at \(t=0\). While we have experimented with other initial conditions, this specific choice corresponds to the physical solution and will therefore provide the best results. The Figure below plots values of \(B[i,0]\) for the initial (blue) and boundary (red) conditions for \(t[0]=0\).

def generate_random_Bo(seed_value):
    np.random.seed(seed_value)  
    random_float = np.random.rand()  
    return random_float

def initial_conditions(N, time_steps, seed_value, m, n, BCtype="neumann"):
    mag_br = generate_random_Bo(seed_value)
    mag_bphi = generate_random_Bo(seed_value + 1)  

    # Extend z to include ghost zones
    z = np.linspace(-1 - 2/N, 1 + 2/N, N+2)
    h = z[1] - z[0]

    Br = np.zeros((N+2, time_steps+1))
    Bphi = np.zeros((N+2, time_steps+1))
    b1 = np.zeros(N)
    b2 = np.zeros(N)

    # Initial Condition for Br and Bphi
    for i in range(1, N+1):
        Br[i, 0] = mag_br * np.sin((m) * np.pi * z[i])
        Bphi[i, 0] = mag_bphi * np.sin((n) * np.pi * z[i])

    # Apply Neumann boundary conditions
    if BCtype == "neumann":
        Br[0, :] = Br[2, :]
        Br[N+1, :] = Br[N -1, :]
        Bphi[0, :] = Bphi[2, :]
        Bphi[N+1, :] = Bphi[N -1, :]

    return z, Br, Bphi, b1, b2, mag_br, mag_bphi

The Implicit Crank-Nicolson Difference Equation#

The implicit Crank-Nicolson difference equation of the Diffusion Equation is given by:

\[ \frac{U_{i,j+1}-U_{i,j}}{k} = \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{U_{i+1j+1}-2U_{ij+1}+U_{i-1j+1}}{h^2}+\frac{U_{i+1j}-2U_{ij}+U_{i-1j}}{h^2}\right) \]

Rearranging the equation, we get:

\[ -\alpha U_{i-1j+1} + ( 2 + 2\alpha)U_{ij+1} - \alpha U_{i+1j+1} = \alpha U_{i-1j} + (2-2\alpha)U_{ij} + \alpha U_{i+1j} \]

for \((i = 1, \ldots, N-1)\), where \((\alpha = \frac{k}{h^2})\) and \(N\) is the grid size.

This can be written in matrix form as:

\[ (I/2 + \alpha B)\mathbf{U}_{j} = (I/2 - \alpha B)\mathbf{U}_{j-1} +\mathbf{b}_{j}+\mathbf{b}_{j+1} \]

for which \(A\) is a \(N-2 \times N-2\) matrix:

\[\begin{split} (2I + \alpha B) = \begin{pmatrix} 2+2\alpha & -\alpha & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ -\alpha & 2+2\alpha & -\alpha & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & -\alpha & 2+2\alpha & -\alpha & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -\alpha & 2+2\alpha & -\alpha & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & -\alpha & 2+2\alpha & -\alpha & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & -\alpha & 2+2\alpha & -\alpha & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & -\alpha & 2+2\alpha & -\alpha \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & -\alpha & 2+2\alpha \\ \end{pmatrix} \end{split}\]

\(B\) is another \(N-2 \times N-2\) matrix:

\[\begin{split} (2I - \alpha B) = \begin{pmatrix} 2-2\alpha & \alpha & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ \alpha & 2-2\alpha & \alpha & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & \alpha & 2-2\alpha & \alpha & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \alpha & 2-2\alpha & \alpha & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & \alpha & 2-2\alpha & \alpha & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & \alpha & 2-2\alpha & \alpha & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & \alpha & 2-2\alpha & \alpha \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & \alpha & 2-2\alpha \\ \end{pmatrix} \end{split}\]

\(\mathbf{V}_j\) is a column vector of size 9 containing \(V_{ij}\) values, \(\mathbf{b}_j\) and \(\mathbf{b}_{j+1}\) are column vectors of size 9 representing the boundary conditions for the current and next time steps, respectively.

Pitch Angle#

Pitch angle \(\alpha\) is the angle between the direction of motion of a charged particle and the direction of the magnetic field it moves in. It’s calculated using the arctangent of the ratio of the radial (\(B_r\)) to the toroidal (\(B_{\phi}\)) magnetic field components:

\[\alpha = \tan^{-1} \left( \frac{B_r}{B_{\phi}} \right)\]

Understanding and controlling pitch angle is crucial in plasma physics, fusion research, and space science for studying particle trajectories, plasma confinement, and stability in magnetic fields.

#  A and B
A = np.zeros((N, N))
B = np.zeros((N, N))
for i in range(N):
    if i == 0 or i == N-1:
        A[i, i] = 1 + r
        B[i, i] = 1 - r
    A[i, i] = 1/2 + 2 * r
    B[i, i] = 1/2 - 2 * r
    if i < N-1:
        A[i, i+1] = -r
        A[i+1, i] = -r
        B[i, i+1] = r
        B[i+1, i] = r
